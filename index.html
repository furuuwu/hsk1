Chinese - HSK1

Since 2026 marks the official rollout of the HSK 3.0 standards, the exam is a bit more robust than it used to be. The new HSK 1 now requires a vocabulary of 300 words (doubled from the old 150) and focuses heavily on "survival" Chinese.

The exam is divided into Listening and Reading. There is currently no writing or speaking section for Level 1, though you must be able to recognize characters (not just Pinyin).

Overview

1. Communication Tasks:

  • Personal Information: Name, age, nationality, family members.
  • Daily Life: Telling time, dates, daily routines.
  • Shopping: Prices, basic items, simple preferences.
  • Environment: Talking about the weather, locations (here/there/where).
  • Interests: Hobbies and simple abilities (like the huì exercises you just did!).

2. Core Grammar Points:

Category Examples
Sentence Structure Subject + Verb + Object (Wǒ hē chá - I drink tea)
Questions Using 吗 (ma) for yes/no; 呢 (ne) for "how about"; 什么 (what).
Negation Using 不 (bù) for present/future; 没有 (méiyǒu) for "don't have".
Measure Words 个 (gè) for people; 本 (běn) for books; 杯 (bēi) for cups.
Possession Using 的 (de) to show ownership (Wǒ de māma).

Sentence structure

Chinese sentence structure is often described as very logical because it follows a strict word order. Unlike English, where you can sometimes move words around for emphasis, or Latin-based languages with complex conjugations, Chinese relies almost entirely on position.

The fundamental "Golden Rule" for Chinese sentences is:
Subject + Time + Location + Manner + Verb + Object

The Basic Foundation (S-V-O):

At its simplest level, Chinese follows the same Subject-Verb-Object pattern as English.

Subject (Who) Verb (Action) Object (What) Translation
我 (wǒ) 喝 (hē) 茶 (chá) I drink tea.
他 (tā) 买 (mǎi) 苹果 (píngguǒ) He buys apples.
我们 (wǒmen) 学习 (xuéxí) 汉语 (Hànyǔ) We study Chinese.

Adding Time and Location:

In English, we usually put time and place at the end of the sentence ("I study Chinese at school on Monday").

In Chinese, you must move them BEFORE the verb. Think of it as setting the stage before the action happens.

  • Time comes after the Subject (or sometimes at the very beginning).
  • Location always uses the word 在 (zài) (at/in) before the place name.

Formula: Subject + Time + Location + Verb + Object

  • English: I drink coffee at home in the morning.
  • Chinese: 我 上午 在家 喝 咖啡。
  • Pinyin: Wǒ shàngwǔ zài jiā hē kāfēi.

Adding Manner (How you do it):

If you want to say how you do something (e.g., "with a friend" or "using a phone"), this also goes before the verb.

  • Formula: Subject + Manner + Verb + Object
  • Example: 我 和朋友 说话。(Wǒ hé péngyou shuōhuà.)
  • Translation: I speak with a friend.

Negation (The "Not"):

To make a sentence negative, you place 不 (bù) or 没 (méi) directly before the verb (or before the auxiliary verb like huì).

  • Positive: 我会说汉语。(I can speak Chinese.)
  • Negative: 我 不 会说汉语。(I cannot speak Chinese.)

Summary Table: Building a Complex Sentence:

Let's build one sentence by adding pieces one by one to see the structure in action:

Structure Level Chinese Sentence Translation
Basic (S-V-O) 我做饭。 I cook.
+ Manner 我用手机做饭。 I cook using a phone (following a recipe).
+ Location 我在家用手机做饭。 I cook using a phone at home.
+ Time 我下午在家用手机做饭。 I cook using a phone at home in the afternoon.

A Quick Trick to Remember:

Imagine you are a movie director.

  1. First, you pick your actor (Subject).
  2. Then, you set the time (Time).
  3. Then, you build the set (Location).
  4. Then, you give them their tools (Manner).
  5. Only then do you yell "Action!" (Verb).

Questions

In Chinese, forming questions is often simpler than in English because you don't have to change the word order. You don't need to "flip" the subject and verb (like "Are you...?" vs "You are...").

There are four main ways to ask questions in HSK 1.

Using the Particle 吗 (ma):

This is for Yes/No questions. You take a normal statement and simply "glue" 吗 to the very end.

  • Statement: 你是老师。(Nǐ shì lǎoshī.) — You are a teacher.
  • Question: 你是老师吗？(Nǐ shì lǎoshī ma?) — Are you a teacher?

More Examples:

  • 你喜欢猫吗？ (Nǐ xǐhuan māo ma?) — Do you like cats?
  • 他在家吗？ (Tā zài jiā ma?) — Is he at home?

Question Words (What, Who, Where):

In English, we move question words to the front ("What is that?"). In Chinese, the question word stays in the exact same spot where the answer would go. This is called "Wh-in-situ."

English Chinese Pinyin Example
What 什么 shénme 这是什么？(What is this?)
Who 谁 shéi / shuí 她是谁？(Who is she?)
Where 哪儿 / 哪里 nǎ'er / nǎlǐ 你在哪儿？(Where are you?)
Which 哪 nǎ 你是哪国人？(Which country are you from?)
How 怎么 zěnme 这个字怎么读？(How do you read this character?)
How many 几 / 多少 jǐ / duōshǎo 你有几个苹果？(How many apples do you have?)

Pro Tip: 几 (jǐ) is used for small numbers (usually 1–10), while 多少 (duōshǎo) is used for larger numbers or prices.

The "How about...?" Particle 呢 (ne):

The particle 呢 is used to bounce a question back to someone else or to ask "Where is [Subject]?" in a very short way.

  • The "Bounce":

  • A: 我很好，你呢？ (Wǒ hěn hǎo, nǐ ne?) — I'm very well, how about you?

  • The "Where is":

  • 我的钱呢？ (Wǒ de qián ne?) — Where is my money? (Equivalent to "Where did it go?" or "Where is it located?")

Verb-Not-Verb (A-not-A) Questions:

This is a more advanced HSK 1 structure where you repeat the verb with a 不 (bù) in the middle. It effectively means "Is it or is it not?"

  • Standard: 你去吗？ (Nǐ qù ma?) — Are you going?
  • A-not-A: 你去不去？ (Nǐ qù bú qù?) — Are you going [or not going]?

Note: If you use this structure, you cannot use "吗" at the end. It's one or the other!

💡 Summary Checklist:

  1. Yes/No? Add 吗 at the end.
  2. Specific Info? Replace the "unknown" word with a Question Word (什么, 谁, etc.).
  3. Returning a question? Use 呢.
  4. Word Order? Keep it exactly the same as a normal sentence!

Negation

In Chinese, negating a sentence is surprisingly straightforward because there are no verb conjugations (no "do not," "does not," "did not," or "hasn't"). You simply place a negative adverb—usually 不 (bù) or 没 (méi)—directly before the verb or adjective.

The trick is knowing which one to use!

Using 不 (bù): General Negation:

不 is used for the present and future, or for things that are habitual or based on your will/opinion.

  • Rule: Subject + 不 + Verb/Adjective
  • Use it for: Facts, habits, future plans, and adjectives.
Context Positive Negative (不) Translation
Fact/Habit 我吃肉。(Wǒ chī ròu.) 我不吃肉。 I don't eat meat.
Future 他明天去学校。 他明天不去学校。 He won't go to school tomorrow.
Will/Ability 我想喝水。 我不想喝水。 I don't want to drink water.
Adjectives 今天很热。(Jīntiān hěn rè.) 今天不热。 Today is not hot.

Using 没 / 没有 (méi / méiyǒu): Past & Possession:

没 is used specifically for the past (things that didn't happen) and for the verb "to have."

  • Rule 1: Subject + 没(有) + Verb (Past Action)
  • Rule 2: Subject + 没(有) + Object (Possession)
Context Positive Negative (没) Translation
Past Action 我看电影了。(Wǒ kàn diànyǐng le.) 我没看电影。 I didn't watch the movie.
Possession 我有钱。(Wǒ yǒu qián.) 我没有钱。 I don't have money.

Crucial Rule: You NEVER use "不" with the verb "有" (to have). You can never say "不有." It is always 没有 (méiyǒu).

The Special Case: "Le" (了):

In HSK 1, you learn that 了 (le) often marks a completed action in a positive sentence. However, when you negate that sentence with 没, the 了 must disappear.

  • Positive: 他去学校了。(He went to school.)
  • Negative: 他没去学校。(He didn't go to school.) ❌ Never say: 他没去学校了。

Summary Table: 不 vs. 没:

Feature 不 (bù) 没 (méi)
Tense Present / Future / Habit Past
Adjectives Used for all (不冷, 不大) Almost never used
With "有" Never Always
With "是" Always (不是) Never
Meaning "I don't/won't" "I didn't/don't have"

Measure words

In English, we sometimes use measure words, like "a cup of coffee" or "two slices of bread." In Chinese, however, every noun requires a measure word when you are counting it or using words like "this" or "that."

You cannot simply say "one book" or "this cat." You must say "one [measure word] book."

The Basic Formula:

Whenever you have a number or a demonstrative (this/that), the measure word (MW) acts as a bridge to the noun.

Formula: Number / This / That + Measure Word + Noun

  • English: Three students.
  • Chinese: 三 个 学生 (Sān gè xuésheng).
  • Literally: Three [individual-unit] students.

The "Must-Know" HSK 1 Measure Words:

While there are hundreds of measure words, you only need a handful for HSK 1.

Measure Word Pinyin Main Use Example
个 gè People, general objects 两个人 (Two people)
本 běn Books, magazines 这本书 (This book)
杯 bēi Cups, glasses 一杯茶 (A cup of tea)
岁 suì Years of age 五岁 (Five years old)
块 kuài Money (basic unit), pieces 十块钱 (Ten yuan)

Why do they exist?:

Think of measure words as "classifiers." They tell you something about the shape or type of the object. For example:

  • 本 (běn) looks like a book with a line through the bottom—it's for things that are bound together.
  • 个 (gè) is the "universal" measure word. If you forget a specific one, you can often use gè and people will still understand you (though it sounds a bit like saying "one stuff of book").

Using "This" and "That":

Even without a number, if you point to something, you need the measure word.

  • 这 (zhè) = This
  • 那 (nà) = That

Examples:

  • 这个苹果 (Zhège píngguǒ) — This apple.
  • 那本书 (Nà běn shū) — That book.
  • 哪个医生？(Nǎ gè yīshēng?) — Which doctor?

Common Pitfall: The Number Two:

When counting things (using a measure word), you don't use the standard number 二 (èr). Instead, you use 两 (liǎng).

  • ❌ Wrong: 二个人 (Èr gè rén)
  • ✅ Right: 两个人 (Liǎng gè rén) — Two people.

Possession

In Chinese, possession is arguably the easiest grammar point to master. You don't have to worry about apostrophes or changing the word "I" to "my"—you just use the "glue" particle: 的 (de).

The Basic Formula:

To show that something belongs to someone, you simply place 的 (de) between the "Owner" and the "Object."

Formula: Owner + 的 + Object

Owner Particle Object Translation
我 (wǒ) 的 书 (shū) My book (Me + 's book)
你 (nǐ) 的 老师 (lǎoshī) Your teacher
爸爸 (bàba) 的 电脑 (diànnǎo) Dad's computer
老师 (lǎoshī) 的 朋友 (péngyou) Teacher's friend

Using "的" with Adjectives:

In HSK 1, 的 isn't just for people owning things; it's also used to connect a describing word (adjective) to a noun.

  • Formula: Adjective + 的 + Noun
  • Example: 漂亮 的 衣服 (Piàoliang de yīfu) — Beautiful clothes.
  • Example: 好 的 老师 (Hǎo de lǎoshī) — A good teacher.

When can you drop the "的"?:

There is one special rule: if you are talking about a very close, personal relationship (like family or your school/country), you can omit the 的 to sound more natural.

  • Family: 我妈妈 (Wǒ māma) — My mom. (Instead of 我的妈妈)
  • Close Relationships: 我朋友 (Wǒ péngyou) — My friend.
  • Institutions: 我们学校 (Wǒmen xuéxiào) — Our school.

The "Whose" Question:

To ask "Whose is this?", you combine the word for "Who" (谁 shéi) with the particle 的.

  • Question: 这是 谁的 书？ (Zhè shì shéi de shū?)
  • Translation: Whose book is this?
  • Answer: 这是 我的 书。 (Zhè shì wǒ de shū.)
  • Translation: This is my book.

Summary Checklist:

  1. Possession? Add 的.
  2. Adjectives? Add 的.
  3. Family member? You can leave it out.
  4. Pronouns? "My," "Your," and "His" are just "I + 的," "You + 的," and "He + 的."
